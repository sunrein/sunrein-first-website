CSS Positioning - 10/18/2015
The differences between static, relative, absolute, and fixed

At the beginning of Week 3, we were prompted to watch a couple videos on CSS. One was over an hour long, and was a presentation entitled "CSS Sucks," subtitled "Life is Hell." While humorous, I didn't take the title too seriously. Hyperbole, I figured, for laughs.

How wrong I was.

One thing I wish I knew going in to CSS was the struggles of positioning. I do not know what my peers would consider the hardest part of working with CSS, but for me, this was definitley the trickiest. Unlike HTML, there are no simple tags for positioning. Rather, we have things like margin, border, and padding, as well as another category of positioning elements: static, relative, absollute, and fixed. I used the latter the most when structuring my site, for better or for worse. I am glad that I was able to figure out what all of these terms mean and what they to help structure the webpage. Whether or not what I did follows best practices, I'm not sure. But this is how these four elements work.

Static

I think that static positioning is the easiest to understand. It does what it says; it's static. This means that it won't move about the page, even if you instruct it to using commands like top, bottom, left, or right. The other nice thing about static positioning is that it is the default status of any element. You don't have to remember to set something to static, you just need to remember to specify something else if you want the element to move.

Relative

Relatively positioned elements behave quite similarly to static ones. However, they can be moved using commands such as top, bottom, left, and right. This is a very precise way to move images around to fit whatever your speficiations are, including having the images overlap one another.

Conclusion

Those are four ways to move things about the page, depending on where you want them to go and what purpose you want them to serve. Static follows the flow of the page and is the default setting. Relative moves things relative to where they would be if they remained static. Absolute is a way of placing something exactly where you want to go on the page, regarless of where it would be statically, and fixed stays in the same spot on the page, even when the page is scrolled or moved around.
